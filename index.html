HELLO

And that’s exactly what we want to convey to our core audience for this film. They too can build their credit and feel good about it. Credit isn’t just a number; it’s a weight off your shoulders. It’s a liberating step in the right direction. That’s exactly the emotion we should be getting from our unique—and quirky—Fingerhut characters.

Honestly, you had me at Chihuahua dumbbells. When we spoke on Zoom, we talked a lot about the spot having a certain energy to it. For me, that needs to come from both the performances as well as the edit and visuals of the spot itself. That's why we respond so positively to the ads that brands like Target create. There's a rhythm, an outlook, an upbeat vibe.

They make you feel like you can be a part of something special.

Tone & Approach

Each scene we dive into needs to be brimming with personality. We’re not seeing a woman mow the lawn in boots as a joke. She’s doing it because that’s who she is—that’s her style. Or when we see our little girl ride her bike with a giant cape behind her, it’s not because her parents just gave her a cape to wear. It’s because she wanted one to own a moment on a suburban sidewalk. In her mind, she’s a model, and this is her runway. The thought being, put a different person in that scene, and you’d have a completely different moment.

To that end, I want each scene to tell a story, no matter how small. We should be able to decipher what the narrative is in one, two, or three shots at the very most. It’s a setup and payoff. For instance, our kitchen couple is having a little contest to see who can work faster. They race around the kitchen, one repairing a cabinet with a drill, the other mixing cake batter. As soon as he puts the mixer in the batter, the batter flies everywhere and they come together, laughing, to give us that slow-motion moment. That’s a two-shot scene in my mind—and it’s full of fun and idiosyncrasy showing us who these people are.

Energy & Slow Motion

I come from a music video and choreography background. I love capturing a distinctive rhythm on camera. In fact, one of my greatest passions is casting former dancers and letting them have fun. There’s so much joy in seeing someone let loose after their energy has been bundled up for so long.

Creating a rhythm and then breaking it up can also be immensely rewarding. That’s what our slow motion will give us. I picture us ramping to slow motion at the exact moment our characters reach their peak confidence. While this could be a burst of dance-inspired movement (during the lawnmower scene), it could just as easily be someone standing powerfully like a superhero (during our grill scene).

Let's make sure we're motivating the slow motion with both big and small moments to really highlight both the product and the eye-catching technique.

But from there, we should then break back into the faster-paced energy of the spot before moving to the next scene. And as we transition, we match the characters’ action between scenes so the rhythm flows into the next moment. For instance, the woman behind the lawnmower does a spin move at the end of her scene. As she twirls, we cut to our guy in the kitchen spinning around his wife. The visual of both characters circling around syncs up to the next scene while maintaining the vibrancy we’ve already established.

Casting

It gets said ad nauseam, but that doesn’t make it any less true: Casting is the most important thing here. While we certainly need people with interesting and diverse looks, we also need people who can make us smile without even seeming to try. For our lawnmower, kitchen couple, laptop teen, and workout woman, I’d love to cast former dancers. Not because we’ll be giving them proper choreography, but rather because they’ll know how to move and express themselves. They’ll naturally radiate happiness. We want that extra bit of flare to their scenes that a dance background will give us. They do something a little bit impressive or do something on the beat and it makes our smile that much bigger.

That said, while we can prescribe what we want these people to do, let’s also allow them to show their true personalities during the audition. If someone can, say, do the worm while pushing a mower, let’s not be afraid to incorporate that. In other words, let’s be open to what the talent can bring, too. It’ll make the result that much more natural.

None of the below is set in stone, but here's how I see the casting so far:

Lawnmower Woman: 50s/60s. African American. It would be fun to have someone with long graying hair or long braids who can whip their hair around (like a lawnmower) as they dance through the yard.

Biker Girl: 8 to 10 years old. Hispanic. She’s not afraid to own the room. She’s bossy and serious about it in the best way. She rides her bike down the sidewalk because she wants the attention.

Kitchen Man: 50s. Asian. I think he should have a hipster/cowboy-inspired look. He’s a rugged hands-on guy who really embraces Americana.

Kitchen Woman: 50s. Hispanic. Short hair, tattoos. Her wardrobe has a retro Rosie the Riveter flare to it. She’s into a ‘50s-inspired pinup culture.

Computer Teenager: 13 to 16 years old. Caucasian or Hispanic. He’s tall and skinny or a bit bigger. Either way, he still has some acne. He’s awkward but that doesn’t make him any less afraid to show his personality online.

Workout Woman: 30s/early 40s. Indian/Southeast Asian. She’s average-looking and is only just just getting into her workout routine.

BBQ Man: 40s. Caucasian or African American. He’s a bigger guy who embraces what he did when he was younger. It would be great to find a drummer—someone who can twirl a BBQ tool around his finger like a drumstick.

CINEMATOGRAPHY

To capture the energy of these spots while still making the people feel real, I want to embrace a controlled handheld look. That means shots can dramatically push into a scene, but with a loose sense of camerawork to them. We’re not just capturing the action; we’re adding to it. Each moment will have a visual panache. This isn’t a documentary camera that’s just a fly on the wall. It’s right there with our actors, enhancing the rush of each frame.

We should also embrace a shallow depth of field when we’re close to our characters. This will make some of the smaller products look great (the sunglasses and boots, for example)—they’ll pop. But it will also give the spot a cinematic aesthetic that makes us feel like we’re right next to real people in real homes.

In terms of lighting, I’m always attached to a sunlit look. It’s bright and beautiful without feeling too overlit. My work for the BBC is a good example of this. The world we're living in is elevated and inviting - but it could just as easily be a real location, and that's due to how it's lit.

LOCATION & ART DIRECTION

Just because you're still working on your credit doesn't mean you can't have style!

We’re after a single location that can give us a variety of different—and colorful—looks. We want to strike a balance with a real house that feels both grounded and slightly aspirational. For this, our best option is an older house that’s been a bit updated. The kitchen is new, but the beautiful wood floors are original. The backyard deck is newly constructed, but there’s a distinct character to the older American Craftsman house just behind it. Our current plan is to shoot this in Chicago, and there’s no doubt in my mind that we’ll find plenty of true-to-life location options there.

After casting, art direction is my second greatest love, and I want to make sure we nail it. We’ll find a good house that will serve as our base, and then dress and paint the walls to finish a specific look. There’s a subtle but coherent style to each environment. As with the images on this page, you can tell each scene has been subtly art-directed. But they’re not done in a way that’s glaring or unrealistic. We think about wall color in relation to wardrobe and negative space.

Our space is also perfectly imperfect: There’s a hodgepodge of grandkid drawings on the fridge, a mess of posters and school papers in the teenager’s room, or a playful piece of art in the woman’s workout area.

THE PRODUCTS

As we discussed on the call, Fingerhut’s main marketing point is to highlight the help it provides in building credit. And as I mentioned before, I think that will certainly come across in how our cast members perform in their respective scenes—you notice it just in their eyes, but also in their physicality. But just as importantly, we want the product to come off as lovingly lit and integrated into the scene. At all times, let’s think about the colors and appearance of the product in relation to our location. I’m not for environments where the color of the drill matches the walls—but I do think color plays a big role in making a product standout. So let’s make sure our products look their absolute best by seamlessly weaving them into the production design of each set.

THE SCRIPT

On the next few pages, I’ve made some suggestions for different locations for certain scenes and different kinds of performances from the actors. But as always, this is all open for discussion!

We open with a shot at grass-level. A lawnmower is approaching the camera, like it’s about to run the lens over. The machine is beautifully backlit, the sun low in the sky. Just behind it—and slightly out of focus—we see someone dancing behind the mower. We cut to a handheld shot of our Lawnmower Woman as she whips her long hair around like helicopter blades. Aside from her Timberland boots, she’s dressed more for a summer party than for yard work. But then—we notice she has headphones on. She’s clearly rocking out, confident and carefree. Her hair slides across the lens, almost touching us.

VO: At Fingerhut, we don’t define you by your credit score.

We cut in closer to her face as we ramp into slow motion. Her long, spinning hair looks incredible in slow motion as she slightly turns to us. She doesn’t look directly into the lens, but rather her own rhythm turns her toward the camera so we can show off her sunglasses. A smile flashes across her face.

VO: We celebrate you being you.

We ramp back to regular motion as she twirls her body. We use her action to match cut a shot of our Kitchen Man also twirling with an electric mixer in hand. A Kitchen Woman with a drill, his wife, counter-spins around him as they trade places. It’s clear they’re having a little race to see who can get their job done first.

[Note: It might be nice to structure this scene as though they’re just moving into their new home. They’re getting a fresh start. Maybe we see moving boxes around the kitchen. Their appliances are new, too, and they’re excited to use them on the new digs.]

VO: That’s why we offer…

Within that same shot, the Man puts his mixer down into the batter and turns it on-here we ramp to slow motion-as cake batter flies everywhere. We ramp back to real time to change focus to our Kitchen Woman who's drilling a screw into a piece of cabinetry, just beyond him. She loks over, laughing, as she throws a kitchen towel at him.

VO: Low monthly payments on the stuff you need...

The kitchen towel match cuts with our Biker Girl's cape as it blows in the wind. She rides confidentally down the sidewalk, owning her little "runway."

VO: … things you’ll love…

We cut to her side as we focus on her boots. We shift to slow motion to show them off. And then cut wide as she abruptly comes to a stop, like she’s a model at the end of her runway. She gives us a fierce glare into the lens as she quickly whips her cape back up at us.

Cut to our Computer Teenager in a retro bathroom. He’s setting up a livestream (maybe he’s a Twitch streamer) and he’s hiding out in the only place he’s allowed to lock the door. He puts a background cloth on the wall to serve as his “set.” We then cut a moment later. Our Computer Teenager dances in front of his laptop, his phone pressed to his face, making him look as though he has cat eyes. We ramp to slow motion here and then back to regular speed as he pulls the phone away from his face.

VO: … and brands you trust…

As the Computer Teenager places down his phone, we use the action to cut to our BBQ Man reaching for a grill tool. We're in a nicely appointed backyard now. We cut wide to reveal our BBQ Man standing proudly at his grill. He's spinning the tool around his finger, like he's a rock drummer waiting for the show to start. We ramp into slow motion as the tool spins around his finger.

VO: … as you build your credit history with a Fingerhut Credit Account.

From him, we cut to our carousel, showcasing all the products Fingerhut offers.

VO: If you’re looking for the latest electronics...(etc.)

We then cut to our Workout Woman in her garage. She's a bit sweaty, like she's deep in the middle of a routine. As she changes to a new exercise, we reveal she's working out with her two Chihuahuas, using them as her dumbbells. Cut in close to one of the dogs at the apex of the dumbbell lift. As its eyes go wide, we ramp to slow motion and then back out again as we...

Cut to our logo and end card.

VO: So you do you and we’ll help build your credit at Fingerhut.com.

EDIT

I’m a huge fan of drawing up animatics so you can get a general yet sharp sense of the tone as we further refine the shots.

It helps me (and everyone on our team!) see what reactions and timings we’re working with. Can we get a longer Partner reaction in one place? Should the edit move a bit faster somewhere else? It’s certainly something I’d do here, too, making sure everything fits within the time we have allotted for live-action scenes.

And if you’d have me, I’d be more than willing to participate in the edit, too, even if it’s just to show you an initial director’s cut.
